Section 1: Writing for Policy Audiences

Academic writing and policy writing are fundamentally different. Academic writing explains methodology in detail and hedges conclusions carefully. Policy writing leads with the answer, provides just enough evidence to be credible, and ends with concrete actions.

The Inverted Pyramid

Policy documents follow an inverted pyramid structure — the most important information comes first:

  1. Executive Summary (1 paragraph): What did you find? What should be done? A busy decision-maker may read only this
  2. Key Findings (2-3 paragraphs with maps): The spatial patterns, hot spots, and temporal trends with visualizations
  3. Recommendations (bulleted list): Specific, actionable steps tied directly to the evidence
  4. Methods and Limitations (brief): Enough for credibility, not a tutorial

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Leading with methods: "We used tidycensus to retrieve ACS 5-year estimates..." — No. Lead with findings
  • Jargon without translation: "Global Moran's I = 0.45 (p < 0.001)" means nothing to a city manager. Instead: "Hardship is strongly concentrated in specific neighborhoods rather than spread evenly across the city"
  • Maps without interpretation: Every map must be accompanied by a sentence that tells the reader what to see
  • Vague recommendations: "The city should address inequality" is not actionable. "The city should prioritize the 12 block groups in south Phoenix identified as persistent hot spots for targeted infrastructure investment" is

Section 2: Quarto Document Features for Professional Output

Quarto extends standard R Markdown with features designed for professional documents. Your policy brief should use these features to improve readability and navigation.

Callout Blocks

Callout blocks highlight important information. Use them for key findings, warnings, or recommendations:

::: {.callout-note}
## Key Finding
Hardship hot spots in south Phoenix have persisted across both ACS vintages,
suggesting entrenched structural disadvantage rather than temporary fluctuation.
:::

Available types: .callout-note, .callout-warning, .callout-important, .callout-tip

Tabsets

Tabsets let readers switch between views without scrolling. Ideal for showing the same geography mapped with different variables or time periods:

::: {.panel-tabset}
## 2015 Hardship
[map code]

## 2023 Hardship
[map code]

## Change
[map code]
:::

Cross-References and Figure Captions

Label figures and tables so you can reference them in text:

#| label: fig-lisa-clusters
#| fig-cap: "LISA cluster map of hardship index, 2019-2023 ACS"

Then reference in text with @fig-lisa-clusters, which renders as "Figure 1" with a hyperlink.

YAML Header for Policy Briefs

Set up your document header for professional output:

---
title: "Neighborhood Hardship in [Your County]"
subtitle: "A Spatial Analysis and Policy Brief"
author: "[Your Name]"
date: today
format:
  html:
    theme: cosmo
    toc: true
    toc-depth: 2
    code-fold: true
    embed-resources: true
---

The code-fold: true option hides code by default but lets technical readers expand it. The embed-resources: true option creates a standalone HTML file with no external dependencies.

Section 4: Formulating Evidence-Based Recommendations

The recommendations section is where your analysis becomes actionable. Each recommendation should follow this pattern:

  1. State the finding: What did the data show?
  2. Explain the implication: Why does this matter for the community?
  3. Propose the action: What specifically should be done, by whom, and where?

Example

"Our LISA analysis identified 14 block groups in the southeast quadrant of the county as persistent hardship hot spots across both ACS vintages. These areas contain 23% of the county's population living below poverty but only 8% of its public transit stops. We recommend that the county transit authority prioritize route expansion in these 14 block groups, starting with connections to the three nearest employment centers identified in our spatial access analysis."

Notice how this recommendation is specific (14 block groups, southeast quadrant), evidence-based (LISA hot spots, persistent over time), and actionable (transit authority, route expansion, specific connections).

Limitations and Honest Uncertainty

Every analysis has limitations. Acknowledge them briefly but directly:

  • ACS data has margins of error, especially at the block group level
  • The hardship index captures only the dimensions you measured — other factors matter
  • Spatial patterns describe where hardship clusters, not why
  • Boundary changes between census vintages may affect temporal comparisons

Acknowledging limitations builds credibility. Ignoring them undermines it.

Lab 06: Policy Brief

In Lab 06, you will synthesize all previous analyses into a professional policy brief. Your deliverable is a single Quarto document that includes:

  1. Executive Summary (1 paragraph): The most important finding and primary recommendation — written so a busy reader can get the message in 30 seconds
  2. Background: Brief context on your study area and why neighborhood hardship matters for policy
  3. Findings: Multi-scale maps and analysis drawn from Labs 1-5, organized as a narrative (not a chronological lab report). Use tabsets for comparative views and callout blocks for key findings
  4. Recommendations (3-5 specific items): Each recommendation must be tied to a specific finding from your analysis, name a responsible entity, and identify a target geography
  5. Limitations: Honest assessment of what the analysis can and cannot tell us

Formatting Requirements

  • Use code-fold: true to hide code by default
  • Include at least one callout block highlighting a key finding
  • Include at least one tabset comparing maps (e.g., 2015 vs. 2023, or original vs. enriched index)
  • Use cross-references for at least two figures
  • Include an interactive mapgl map so readers can explore neighborhoods
  • Use embed-resources: true so the HTML file is self-contained

Connection to all previous labs: This is the capstone. You are assembling the measurement (Lab 1), cartography (Lab 2), spatial integration (Lab 3), hot spot analysis (Lab 4), and temporal analysis (Lab 5) into a single coherent document. The quality of your brief depends on the quality of all prior work.
