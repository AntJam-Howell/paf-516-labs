Yellowdig Discussion

Neighborhood change is often framed as either “improvement” or “decline,” but these labels depend heavily on whose perspective you center. A block group where poverty drops from 35% to 15% over eight years could represent genuine upward mobility for long-time residents — or it could signal displacement, with the original community replaced by wealthier newcomers while the poverty simply moved elsewhere.

This tension sits at the heart of temporal analysis with census data. The numbers show what changed, but they cannot tell us who benefited or who was harmed. Freeman (2005) found that displacement rates in gentrifying neighborhoods were not dramatically higher than in non-gentrifying ones, yet Ding, Hwang, and Divringi (2016) showed that when vulnerable residents do move from gentrifying areas, they tend to land in lower-income neighborhoods — a finding invisible in aggregate census statistics.

Discussion prompt:

Drawing on this week’s readings and your own change map, discuss the policy implications of temporal change analysis.

  • Should public investment target neighborhoods where hardship is currently highest, or neighborhoods where hardship is increasing fastest? What are the trade-offs of each approach?
  • Use at least one specific example — from the readings, from your lab results, or from a real Maricopa County community — to ground your argument.
  • Consider what census data can and cannot reveal about whether neighborhood change benefits existing residents or displaces them.
