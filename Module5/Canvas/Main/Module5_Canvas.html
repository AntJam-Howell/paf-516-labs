Temporal Change Analysis

Module Overview and Objectives

Modules 1–4 analyzed your study area at a single point in time. But neighborhoods are not static — they gentrify, decline, stabilize, or fluctuate over years and decades. This module adds the time dimension to your analysis by comparing economic hardship index values across two ACS 5-year vintages: the 2009–2013 ACS and the 2015–2019 ACS. These two periods bracket the entire post-recession decade in Maricopa County — 2013 captures neighborhoods still in mid-recovery of the housing crisis, and 2019 captures the peak of the recovery just before COVID disrupts everything. That is a substantively compelling policy window.

Temporal analysis with census data introduces unique methodological challenges. Census boundaries can change between decennial censuses. Z-score indices must be standardized on a common scale before comparing them across years. Margins of error can make small changes statistically meaningless. This module addresses all of these issues while building directly on the index and spatial skills from previous labs.

After completing this module, you will be able to:

  • Retrieve and compare ACS data from two different vintages using tidycensus
  • Understand why boundary vintage consistency is essential for direct comparison — and how choosing years that share the same boundaries avoids the problem entirely
  • Apply pooled standardization to produce z-scores that are comparable across time periods
  • Compute change scores for a composite hardship index
  • Construct a MOE-based significance test to distinguish real change from sampling noise
  • Apply diverging color scales to communicate the direction and magnitude of change
  • Evaluate the temporal stability of spatial patterns identified in Module 4

Section 1: Choosing the Right Time Period

The American Community Survey (ACS) provides rolling 5-year estimates. The tidycensus package lets you pull data from any available vintage by setting the year argument in get_acs(). For temporal comparison, the most important constraint is geographic consistency.

The Boundary Vintage Problem

Census block group boundaries are redrawn with each decennial census (2010, 2020). This means:

  • ACS vintages 2013–2019 use 2010 TIGER/Line boundaries
  • ACS vintages 2020–present use 2020 TIGER/Line boundaries

If you compare a year on 2010 boundaries to a year on 2020 boundaries, many GEOIDs will not match — some block groups were split, merged, or renumbered. An inner_join() on GEOID silently drops these CBGs, creating a map full of holes (missing units) that look like missing data but are actually a methodological artifact.

The solution is simple: compare two years that share the same boundary vintage. Using year = 2013 and year = 2019 gives you 2006–2010 and 2015–2019 ACS data, both on 2010 TIGER boundaries. Every GEOID in the 2010 file exists in the 2019 file — the inner join works cleanly and the map is complete.

Variable Consistency Across Vintages

A second constraint is that census table structures change across years. Lab 5 uses a 4-variable index — poverty rate, unemployment rate, share without a high school diploma, and median household income — all drawn from tables that have been consistently available across ACS vintages. The health insurance variable used in Labs 1–4 (B27010_017) did not exist in the 2010 ACS, so it is dropped here. This is itself a lesson: real analysts routinely adjust their indicator sets when building cross-time comparisons.

Section 2: Pooled Standardization

When comparing z-score-based indices across time, you must decide: do you standardize each year’s data using that year’s mean and standard deviation, or using a pooled mean and standard deviation across both years combined?

Why Year-Specific Standardization Fails for Change Analysis

If you standardize each year separately, a z-score of 1.0 in 2010 and a z-score of 1.0 in 2019 both mean “one standard deviation above that year’s mean” — but the absolute level of hardship could be very different. Phoenix in 2010 was at the trough of the worst housing crisis in modern history. Poverty rates, unemployment rates, and uninsured rates were all elevated. If you standardize 2010 data to mean 0, you are hiding the fact that 2010 was a genuinely worse year on average. Subtracting the two standardized indices would then tell you that most neighborhoods “had no change” — because you’ve already removed the year-level mean difference.

The Pooled Approach

Pooled standardization computes a single mean and standard deviation from the combined 2010 and 2019 observations together, then applies that common scale to both years. Now a z-score of 1.0 in 2010 and a z-score of 1.0 in 2019 mean the same thing: “one standard deviation above the combined average.” Change scores computed from pooled z-scores are directly interpretable as movements on a common scale.

Key insight: Pooled standardization preserves real year-level shifts in the data. If 2010 truly had higher poverty than 2019 on average, the pooled z-scores will reflect that — and the change scores will capture genuine improvement.

Section 3: Computing Change Scores and Testing Significance

The Change Score

The change score is simply the difference between the period 2 and period 1 hardship indices:

  • Positive change: Hardship increased (neighborhood deteriorated over the decade)
  • Negative change: Hardship decreased (neighborhood improved over the decade)
  • Near zero: Neighborhood was stable

Because we are using pooled z-scores, a change score of +0.5 means “moved half a pooled standard deviation toward greater hardship” — a substantively interpretable magnitude.

Testing Significance with Margins of Error

ACS estimates come with margins of error (MOE), and these margins can be substantial for small geographies like block groups. A change from 15% to 18% poverty may look meaningful on a map, but if both estimates have MOEs of ±5 percentage points, the “change” is well within sampling noise.

The Census Bureau provides guidance for computing a z-test for the difference between two ACS estimates: z = (estimate₂ − estimate₁) / √(SE₁² + SE₂²), where SE = MOE / 1.645 (for 90% confidence intervals). If |z| > 1.645, the change is statistically significant at the 90% level.

Why it matters: In Lab 5, you will compute this test for the poverty rate (the most interpretable individual variable) and use it to distinguish statistically significant change from noise. Block groups where change is significant will be highlighted on the map.

Section 4: Diverging Color Scales for Change Maps

Change data has a meaningful midpoint (zero = no change), which calls for a diverging color palette rather than the sequential viridis palette used in previous labs. Diverging palettes use two distinct hues radiating from a neutral center:

  • Blue → White → Red: Common convention where red signals alarm (increasing hardship) and blue signals improvement. This is the convention used in Lab 5 — consistent with the traffic-light metaphor most audiences already know.
  • Purple → White → Orange: A colorblind-friendly alternative with similar visual logic.

Critical Design Decisions

Center the midpoint on zero. The neutral color must correspond to “no change,” not the median of your data. In ggplot2, use scale_fill_gradient2(midpoint = 0) — not the default which centers on the data mean.

Use symmetric limits. Set limits = c(-max_abs, max_abs) where max_abs = max(abs(change_df$hardship_change)). This ensures the visual weight of positive and negative change is balanced — a map that goes from -0.5 to +2.0 will visually exaggerate worsening even if most change is small.

Handle outliers with winsorizing. Extreme values compress the rest of the scale. Cap values at the 2nd and 98th percentiles before mapping so that a few outlier block groups don’t wash out the signal for everyone else.

  • Freeman, L. (2005). Displacement or succession? Residential mobility in gentrifying neighborhoods. Urban Affairs Review, 40(4), 463–491. Link — Seminal study examining whether gentrification causes displacement; finding that demographic change is driven more by the relative affluence of in-movers than by displacement of existing residents.

Recent Applications

  • Landis, J. D. (2016). Tracking and explaining neighborhood socioeconomic change in U.S. metropolitan areas between 1990 and 2010. Housing Policy Debate, 26(1), 2–52. Link — Large-scale empirical study measuring gentrification and socioeconomic neighborhood change across U.S. metros using census tract data over two decades.
  • Ding, L., Hwang, J., & Divringi, E. (2016). Gentrification and residential mobility in Philadelphia. Regional Science and Urban Economics, 61, 38–51. Link — Shows that when vulnerable residents move from gentrifying areas, they tend to relocate to lower-income neighborhoods — a finding invisible in aggregate census statistics.

Census Data and Methods

  • U.S. Census Bureau. Comparing ACS Data — Official guidance on making valid comparisons across ACS vintages, including boundary changes and margins of error.
  • Spielman, S. E., Folch, D. C., & Nagle, N. N. (2014). Patterns and causes of uncertainty in the American Community Survey. Applied Geography, 46, 147–157. DOI — Systematic analysis of where and why MOEs are largest in small-geography ACS data. Essential reading for understanding when change scores are reliable.

Key Terms

Term Definition
ACS Vintage A specific 5-year period of American Community Survey data (e.g., 2015–2019, labeled year = 2019 in tidycensus)
Boundary Vintage The decennial census year whose TIGER/Line boundaries underlie a given ACS vintage (2010 or 2020)
Pooled Standardization Standardizing two years of data against a single combined mean and SD, so z-scores are directly comparable across time
Change Score The difference between a variable’s value at two time points (period 2 minus period 1)
Margin of Error (MOE) The range around an ACS estimate within which the true value likely falls at 90% confidence
Significance Test for Change z = (est₂ − est₁) / √(SE₁² + SE₂²); if |z| > 1.645, the change is significant at 90%
Diverging Color Scale A color palette with two hues radiating from a neutral midpoint; appropriate when data has a meaningful center (zero change)
Winsorizing Capping extreme values at a specified percentile to prevent outliers from compressing the color scale
Temporal Stability The degree to which spatial patterns (e.g., LISA hot spots) persist across time periods
Gentrification Neighborhood change characterized by rising incomes, property values, and demographic shifts — often at the expense of existing lower-income residents
